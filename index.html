Get to know me

About me

I'm Erin — a Product Designer based in Charleston, SC with a background that's equal parts psychology, information science, and genuine curiosity about how people make sense of complex systems.

I studied Psychology and Information Science at USC, then earned my UX Design & Research certification from Flatiron School and a UI Design certification from Shift Nudge in 2025. That intersection of human behavior and information architecture is where I live — it's what makes me obsessive about clarity, hierarchy, and flow.

When I'm not designing, I'm usually being supervised by my three cats: Gimli, Nala, and Stella. They have strong opinions about my work.

"I believe great design happens where clarity, collaboration, and curiosity meet."

Design Philosophy

Design isn't just about making things look good — it's about making things work better for people. My philosophy is rooted in the belief that the best solutions emerge from rigorous research, honest collaboration, and a relentless commitment to simplicity on the other side of complexity.

Six principles I design by

Information hierarchy isn't a visual exercise — it's a cognitive one. When structure is right, users don't have to think. They just find. I obsess over how content is organized so that every screen tells a coherent story from top to bottom.
The first version is a hypothesis. I treat every design artifact as a test — from rough sketches to polished prototypes — staying attached to outcomes, not outputs. Iteration isn't a sign of failure; it's the process working correctly.
Qualitative research tells me what and why. Quantitative data tells me how much. Together, they give me the full picture needed to make confident design decisions — and to prove the impact of those decisions to stakeholders.
I thrive at the intersection of complexity and elegance. Healthcare systems, government portals, connected device ecosystems — these are inherently messy domains. My job is to find the threads of logic inside the chaos and surface them as clear, trustworthy experiences.
Design gets better when designers get better. I invest in mentorship, design critique culture, and building frameworks that make the whole team stronger — not just my own projects.
Good design should be measurable. I track outcomes — conversion rates, task success rates, sentiment scores, support call deflection — because impact is what separates design that looks great from design that works.

Design Process & Workshops

How I turn chaos into clarity

A behind-the-scenes look at the methods, workshops, and frameworks I use to move from fuzzy problem to polished product — every time.

The five-phase process
01

Discover & Understand

Start with questions, not answers. Exploratory research, competitive analysis, user interviews, and surveying establish the full landscape before any design decisions are made.

Discovery ResearchCompetitive AnalysisUser InterviewsSurveying
02

Synthesis & Design

Raw research becomes actionable insight. Problem statements, design principles, personas, and research artifacts give the team a shared foundation to design from.

Problem StatementsDesign PrinciplesPersonasResearch Artifacts
03

Concept Design

Divergent thinking before convergent thinking. Style tiles, mood boards, sketching, and parallel concept development explore the possibility space before narrowing in.

Style TilesMood BoardsSketchingDivergent Concepts
04

Prototype & Build

Low, mid, and high fidelity prototypes bring concepts to life — giving stakeholders something tangible and giving users something to react to.

Lo-Fi WireframesMid-Fi DesignsHi-Fi PrototypesSite Architecture
05

Test & Iterate

Designs are hypotheses until tested. Usability testing, iteration cycles, and final delivery ensure that what ships is what users actually need.

Usability TestingIterationsStyle GuideFinal Deliverables
Activities & Methods

The toolkit I reach for

These are the research and design activities I use most — each chosen deliberately based on what the project needs, the team size, and the phase we're in.

Synthesis

Affinity Mapping

Discover patterns across a wide set of ideas and notes by grouping items by similarity and themes. Use dot voting to prioritize emerging clusters.

1–20 peopleUp to 4 hrsPost-research
IA & Research

Card Sorting

Discover patterns across topics and define category structures that match user mental models. Four steps: determine items, time-boxed sorting, name each group, determine frequency.

1–20 people15–60 min sort5–20 min review
Problem Framing

How Might We

Reframe pain points as questions and determine action steps. Write pain points, group them, reframe as HMW questions, then generate solutions.

1–20 peopleUp to 4 hrsDiscovery → Define
Research Tracking

KWL Framework

Track what you Know, Want to know, and have Learned throughout a project. Especially useful for identifying knowledge gaps and steering research priorities.

1–5 peopleOngoingAny phase
Prioritization

MoSCoW Method

Prioritize features into Must Have, Should Have, Could Have, and Won't Have categories — creating alignment between design, product, and engineering on what matters most.

1–5 people1–2 hrsPost-ideation
User Modeling

Personas

Create composite user personas from research and interviews across three steps: research, determine user types, and build personas that keep the team anchored to real human needs.

1–10 people6–13 hrs totalDefine phase
Inspiration

Lightning Demos

Share design inspiration from websites and apps — each person presents and discusses their picks. Fast, energizing, and great for cross-pollinating ideas across industries.

1–10 peopleFlexibleDesign kickoff
Ideation

Story Boarding & Sketching

Expand a specific idea to build understanding and a common vision, framing the experience holistically. Fold paper, time-box individual creation, draw context and content, review as a group.

1–6 people15–20 min gen5–20 min review
Prioritization

Priority Matrix

Evaluate impact vs. effort of features or flows. Determine items, then time-box placement into four quadrants to surface what to build first, next, or never.

1–5 people1–2 hrsPost-ideation
Workshop Frameworks

Five types of workshops, one goal

Discovery Workshops

Create alignment on overall goals, scope, and milestones. Gather team-specific information to ensure everyone starts from the same foundation.

Design Workshops

Foster collaborative culture, generate a wide range of ideas, and refine chosen design directions. The best designs come from rooms where everyone has a voice.

Empathy Workshops

Shift mindsets to user-first. Share prior user knowledge and convince stakeholders of necessary changes through the lens of real user needs.

Critique Workshops

Objectively review existing designs and assess progress against criteria, principles, and user needs — without ego, with intention.

Prioritization Workshops

Align stakeholders around business goals, bring in users to determine feature value, and manage scope creep after design has begun.
